# Silver Criteria for Research Groups

Version: 1.0

## Regularly discuss digital sustainability in group meetings

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-general-blue)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20G1--S-white)

### Impact
Ensures continuity in sustainability efforts and commitment to long-term sustainability goals.

### Description
Making space in group meetings for digital sustainability establishes a shared continuous commitment to embedding sustainability efforts in group activities. Adding it to the agenda ensures it doesn’t get forgotten. Sustainability doesn't have to dominate a meeting, but this provides a space for discussion to ensure long-term success.

**How?**

Create a regularly occurring (at least monthly) agenda item for digital sustainability in group meetings. Get/provide approval from senior management to ensure buy-in.

**Building on Bronze** 

Provide evidence showing that a Green DiSC representative is still nominated from your group. If the previous representative identified in your Bronze application is no longer with your group, make sure to provide evidence that this role has been passed on to someone else.

### Checklist
- **1) Identify what meetings are best suited for this item and obtain buy-in from the group’s leader (obligatory)**
- **2) Add a recurring agenda item on digital sustainability to the group’s meetings (obligatory)**
    - Provide evidence of digital sustainability having been included in meeting discussions (e.g. screenshot, email, minutes of meeting agenda)
- **3) Provide evidence on your current Green DiSC representative (highlight if this role has been transitioned to someone else since your Bronze application) (obligatory)**

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That there is a dedicated section of group/lab meeting agendas for discussion around digital sustainability issues and initiatives.
- That these meetings occur at least monthly.
- That you still have a Green DiSC representative, who may be someone new since your Bronze application if the last one has left the role.

**Evidence can include:**
- Screenshot of meeting schedules.
- Copy of meeting minutes (you can black out parts that aren’t relevant).
- Screenshot of email trail documenting agenda and calendar invite.
- Screenshot of your current Green DiSC representative’ profile identifying them as such to the rest of the lab.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence of digital sustainability (not just generic sustainability initiatives on site) included in group/lab meetings?
- Is there evidence of these meetings occurring regularly (i.e. at least monthly)?
- Is there evidence that they still have a nominated Green DiSC representative?

## Train all group members in sustainable computing
![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-general-blue)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20G2--S-white)

### Impact
Ensures that the green computing induction developed in Bronze has now benefitted all group members, even the ones who have joined before it was introduced. 

### Description
Every member of the group should complete training dedicated to green computing to ensure common knowledge across the group. This applies to new starters who have received the induction material developed in Bronze, and long-time staff who will be trained as well.

**How?**

You can either run a training session with the entire group, or share existing on-demand courses (examples below), and ask group members  to confirm that they have completed it. Provide evidence that this is done for all members of the group.

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that induction procedures continue to include computing sustainability within scope, through specific training on digital and computing sustainability, and that these materials are made available to new starters in the group.

### Checklist
- **1) Identify suitable training material adapted to your group (obligatory).**
    - Discuss with team members the best way for everyone to upskill in this space.
    - Plan the delivery of the training.
- **2) Promote this training to the team and track members training status (obligatory).**
    - Provide evidence that all members of your group have completed green computing training
- **3) Provide evidence that your induction materials still cover computing sustainability (obligatory).**

### Resources and Case Studies
You can use some of these online on-demand training courses: the [SPARKhub Training: Emissions of Digital Research Infrastructure](https://sdratraining.sparkhub.eu/) which is quite short, or [EMBL-EBI’s Sustainable computing in science](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/training/online/courses/sustainable-computing-in-science/) training for a slightly deeper deep-dive.

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- Induction procedures continue to include specific training dedicated to green computing.
- This induction material is accessible to all new starters of your group.
- That all members of your group have completed the green computing training.

**Evidence can include:**
- Screenshots or PDF of the induction material.
- Certifications, screenshots showing course completion per member, or a list of member names with dates of completion and (digital) signatures.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that induction procedures still include training dedicated to green computing?
- Is there evidence showing that all members of the group have completed this training?

## Embed sustainability in teaching delivered by the group

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-general-blue)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20G5--S-white)

### Impact
Teaching is a great opportunity to promote sustainable actions to students and peers. 

### Description
If the group conducts training or teaching activities, they should include some mentions of relevant sustainable computing  elements. This does not apply if the group focuses purely on research activities and does not develop, or deliver any training.

**How?**

Embed environmental sustainability considerations into training material delivered by team members. These elements can be inspired by the training elements you have included in your induction process, and here again, feel free to use open material developed by others (e.g. the [EMBL-EBI](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/training/online/courses/sustainable-computing-in-science/) and [SPARKhub courses](https://sdratraining.sparkhub.eu/) are both under CC-BY licences).


### Checklist
- **1) Identify teaching sessions delivered by the team that have computing components (obligatory)**
- **2) Include elements of digital sustainability in the material, e.g. by using existing content (obligatory).**
    - Provide screenshots/slides of the sustainability content embedded in the teaching materials disseminated by the group (e.g. training courses, workshops, best practices documentation)
    - Provide a list of the sessions delivered that have included this material already.

### Resources and Case Studies
You can use some of these online on-demand training courses: the [SPARKhub Training: Emissions of Digital Research Infrastructure](https://sdratraining.sparkhub.eu/) which is quite short, or [EMBL-EBI’s Sustainable computing in science](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/training/online/courses/sustainable-computing-in-science/) training for a slightly deeper deep-dive.

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That digital sustainability is embedded in the training activities of your group.
- That these materials are disseminated and are made accessible to users outside the group (e.g. students, other members of the institution).

**Evidence can include:**
- Copy of training materials (e.g. screenshot, PDF).
- Screenshot of emails, resource repository, posts and/or announcements of the training materials being disseminated.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that sustainable computing is covered in the group’s training materials?
- Are these training materials effectively disseminated to users outside the group?

## Request Life Cycle Assessments for the purchase of new IT equipment

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-offices-yellow)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20O1--S-white)

### Impact
Knowing the environmental impacts of different models is important to make informed purchasing decisions. Ensures future purchasing has sustainability embedded in choices, and increases pressure on  suppliers to provide LCAs.

### Description
Life cycle assessments (LCAs) are standardised measures of the total environmental impacts of a product, covering manufacturing, transportation, operations and disposal. They allow for comprehensive comparisons between the environmental impacts of different products but are still too rarely part of procurement decisions.

While as a group you have limited influence over the procurement process of your institution, you are still in a position to request these and raise this issue to influence institutional policies.

When procuring new IT equipment of any kind, you should reach out to your central procurement team (if procuring through the institution) or to the manufacturer (if procuring directly) and request to know the lifecycle environmental impacts of the different models you consider.

**How?**

Request the LCAs of the IT products you consider purchasing either through the central procurement team or external vendors. In addition, you can request the sustainability credentials of the company producing them (e.g. ISO standards, environmental policies).

*📣 Ask your Central Team! If your central team has applied or is applying to Green DiSC as well, you should be able to ask them about the evidence they provided for this criterion and what relevant information is available for you to include in your application.*

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that the hardware inventory from your Bronze application is updated, noting used and unused laptops, desktop computers, monitors, tablets, and external hard drives.

### Checklist
- **1) Identify whether LCAs are already included in the procurement platform you use (obligatory)**
    - If yes, make sure to consider it as part of procurement decisions.
- **2) Request the LCA of all new IT equipment as part of the procurement process (obligatory)**
    - If successful, provide examples of LCAs obtained from a recent purchase of IT equipment.
    - If unsuccessful, provide evidence that this has been requested and that the procurement teams were made aware of this increasing demand for this (ideally with an idea of when it will be implemented institution-wide).
- **3) Provide evidence that your hardware inventory submitted during your Bronze application is being maintained, noting the date of last update (obligatory).**

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That an LCA was requested with recent purchases of IT equipment (through the central team if applicable) and whether it was successful.
- That the IT equipment inventory is kept up-to-date.

**Evidence can include:**
- Screenshot of communications with external vendor or central team requesting the LCAs and details of the institution’s position on this.
- Screenshot or PDF of the LCAs, if available.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that the group had requested for an LCA with a recent purchase of IT equipment, whether directly to the supplier or through their central team?
- Is there evidence of the obtained LCAs, if available?
- Importantly, it’s okay if the group didn’t manage to obtain the LCA (as they only have limited leverage on this), what matters is that they tried and raised the issue with the relevant parties.

## “Reuse, Repair, (Repurpose,) Recycle” is now common practice for IT equipment

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-offices-yellow)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20O2--S-white)

### Impact
Reduces e-waste and how much new equipment is purchased.

### Description
The manufacturing of IT equipment is a significant share of their environmental impacts and reducing how much new hardware we purchase mitigates this.  E-waste, on the other hand, is often disposed of improperly, e.g. in landfills, contributing to the leeching of toxic chemicals in the environment. It is important to ensure that as much equipment can be reused, or its materials be recovered or repurposed to help reduce extraction of new materials and resources. This helps close the loop, contributing to a more circular economy, and helps others in need of the equipment.

**How?**

The group has a process in place to extend the life expectancy of IT equipment and clear repurposing streams for equipment it no longer needs. It also has a process ensuring that all e-waste is adequately recycled. If no institutional process exists for these two streams, the group has identified suitable alternatives they can use and have started using them. 

For example, you can engage with suppliers during procurement for information on the durability of the equipment and the return policy after usage. You can also seek out local initiatives, e.g. charities, which can accept unused equipment.

*📣 Ask your Central Team! If your central team has applied or is applying to Green DiSC as well, you should be able to ask them about the evidence they provided for this criterion and what relevant information is available for you to include in your application.*

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that builds on what you have gathered in Bronze, filling knowledge gaps with the e-waste system from your institution, and providing more detailed information on this process.

### Checklist
- **1) Provide details of the repair, reuse, and repurpose processes available to your team (obligatory)**
    - In particular, identify what to do with functioning equipment your team no longer needs.
- **2) Provide a list of facilities for recycling e-waste and details about the process ensuring suitable recycling (obligatory)**
- **3) Provide evidence that this information is available to members and has been used (obligatory)**

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That there is a clear and known system in place for processing electronic waste and unused functioning IT equipment. Note that simply saying “company X is in charge of recycling for the university” isn’t sufficient, and you should provide details on what the processes entail and why they are environmentally friendly.
- That there is a clear and explicit source of information (e.g. contact person, department, website) for these processes and that this is accessible to members.

**Evidence can include:**
- Description of the processes.
- Screenshot of the institutional websites covering these two elements (if any).
- Screenshot of contact details of the point person and/or department responsible for this system and its dissemination in the institution.
- Screenshot of the website/s of external vendors involved in the process.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there sufficient evidence describing the process for repurposing IT equipment, and evidence it’s been used?
- Is there sufficient evidence describing the process for recycling e-waste, and evidence it’s been used?
- Is there evidence showing detail to support that the group is aware of this system/processes?

## Implement data management plans for the major data resources controlled

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-data%20storage-red)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20DS1--S-white)

### Impact
Enables sustainable data management and deletion of unnecessary data. Ensures the group has a shared understanding of these practices.

### Description
Data is a precious commodity, and it is important to have sufficient back ups available to avoid data loss. Simultaneously, excessive data storage (e.g. storing too many copies of large files) also comes with unnecessary carbon footprints. If designed properly, maintained up-to-date, and implemented, data management plans (DMPs) address both issues by ensuring an appropriate number of copies of each dataset is kept, and that unnecessary data is deleted.

**How?**

Create a data management plan for all major data resources (you can use the inventory created when applying for Bronze), you can use online resources to guide you. This should address data conservation as well as data deletion. The entire group is made aware of this and it’s the group’s policy to follow this plan.

In your DMP, it is useful to provide a justification for the number of copies maintained and the expiry date of the data (i.e. when it can be deleted). In particular, the number of copies is controlled, wherein enough copies are present for appropriate back-ups and version control.

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that the inventory of data resources submitted with your Bronze application is still being maintained, noting the date of last update.

### Checklist
- **1) Identify data that the group controls and that should have DMPs (obligatory)**
- **2) Write and provide detail of the data management plans for each (obligatory)**
    - Provide evidence of this plan being shared to the group
- **3) Provide evidence showing that your data resource inventory from your Bronze application is maintained, noting the date of last update. (obligatory)**

### Resources and Case Studies
- You can use [this resource as a DMP template](https://dmponline.dcc.ac.uk/), but there are other options available online.

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That you have prepared data management plans for the data resources you manage.
- That this data management plan is shared to your group and is easily accessible.
- That you have maintained a data resource inventory.

**Evidence can include:**
- Your data management plans.
- A screenshot and supporting description of how this data management plan is accessible.
- A spreadsheet of your data resource inventory.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that the group has prepared  data management plans?
- Is there supporting evidence showing that this plan is easily accessible by members of the group?
- Is the group’s data resource inventory maintained since their Bronze application?

## Regular data cleaning is now common practice

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-data%20storage-red)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20DS2--S-white)

### Impact
Ensures sustainable data management by having enough back-ups but also deleting outdated or unnecessary data.

### Description
It is good practice to ensure you are not storing data that is no longer relevant, valid, or needed – especially for datasets that take particularly large space. This helps minimise the environmental costs of your data storage moving forward. This process established in Bronze should be recurring and standard policy now.

**How?**

The process established in Bronze is implemented regularly, once or twice a year is ideal. Meetings have been scheduled within the group, with the relevant parties responsible for reviewing datasets. Expired datasets that are no longer needed are deleted.

### Checklist
- **1) Review data cleaning process established in Bronze, update it if necessary (obligatory)**
- **2) Collect evidence of past occurrences of data cleaning (obligatory)**
    - Evidence of a yearly review being scheduled to discuss data cleaning
    - Evidence of at least 2 previous occurrences of data cleaning with supporting detail (e.g. space freed) (these can happen more than once a year of course).

### Resources and Case Studies
- A guide for organising data cleaning can be found [here](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1013791).

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That data cleaning has been implemented as a regularly occurring practice within your group, and not just a one-off event.
- That group-wide data cleaning has occurred at least twice.
- That the data cleaning process is maintained and accessible.

**Evidence can include:**
- Exported data through a table or a screenshot of data freed from the previous occurrences of data cleaning.
- Screenshot of emails announcing the scheduled data cleaning event in the group.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence of data cleaning having occurred at least twice in the past?
- Is there sufficient evidence showing the impact of the data cleaning event (e.g. space freed)?
- Is there evidence supporting that the data cleaning event is established as a regularly occurring event within the group?

## Review the computing infrastructures used

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-compute-silver)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20C1--S-white)

### Impact
Ensures that sustainable infrastructure choices are made when possible and allows us to reconsider high-carbon choices.

### Description
Conducting a review of the computing infrastructures used by your group (e.g. HPC, powerful desktop computer, the group's own server rack in the office) is important to ensure that these balance research needs with sustainability in a satisfactory way.

Oftentimes, using shared computing infrastructures in your institution is a more efficient option than private hardware. If these options are not available or suitable, understanding roadblocks can help your group consider potential interim solutions that can lead to transitioning to more sustainable solutions in the future.

**How?**

Based on the inventory of computing infrastructures started in Bronze, updated if necessary, assess the environmental impacts of each infrastructure (e.g. estimating a rough annual energy usage and carbon footprint) as well as level of usage. For the less efficient infrastructures, list the roadblocks to moving computing activities and review the feasibility of these.

You can obtain rough estimates with online tools such as the [Green Algorithms calculator](http://calculator.green-algorithms.org). If relevant, you can consult your institution’s computing teams to see if there is guidance on how to leverage efficient shared infrastructure.

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that the computing infrastructure inventory developed in your Bronze application is maintained and updated, citing the date of last update.

### Checklist
- **1) Provide evidence showing that the computing infrastructure inventory from your Bronze application is maintained, noting the date of last update (obligatory)**
- **2) Review whether some more sustainable infrastructure choice could be made (obligatory)**
    - Provide examples of a review conducted, with carbon footprint numbers
    - If appropriate, detail the plan to switch to more efficient computing infrastructure or list roadblocks with recommendations for solutions or future alternatives

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That a review of computing infrastructures used by the group was conducted, with supporting detail on each (e.g. type, carbon footprint).
- That a plan was designed to transition to more sustainable alternatives if appropriate, with clear description of any roadblocks, recommendations, and next steps.
- That the computing infrastructure inventory is maintained, noting the date of last update.

**Evidence can include:**
- A list of computing infrastructures used by the group, with additional columns listing supporting detail for each one.
- A document detailing the sustainability transition plan moving forward.
- The updated computing infrastructure inventory.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that a thorough review of computing infrastructures used by the group was conducted?
- Is there supporting detail for each infrastructure detailed in this list?
- Is there evidence of a sustainability transition plan designed, with detail of roadblocks faced (if any), recommendations, and next steps?

## Estimate energy usage and environmental impacts of major workflows

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-compute-silver)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20C2--S-white)

### Impact
Informs on the resource usage of the main computing tools used in the groups, highlights hotspots, and helps prioritise improvements.

### Description
Using the catalog of computing workflow hotspots conducted for Bronze, and updated if necessary, estimate the energy usage and associated environmental impacts of each major workflow. 

**How?**

A number of resources exist to help you track the environmental impacts of individual analyses (listed below). You can use online calculators, embed packages in the code, or use monitoring tools available on the computing platform you use. Completing the other Silver criteria “Monitor and report computing usage and environmental impacts” can also help with this.

**Building on Bronze**

Provide evidence that the computing pipeline inventory developed in your Bronze application is maintained and updated, citing the date of last update.

### Checklist
- **1) Update the list of computing workflow hotspots created for Bronze (obligatory)**
- **2) For each one, estimate resource usage and environmental impacts for the use-cases of the group (obligatory)**
    - You should at least include: compute resources (e.g. core-hours and memory), energy usage, carbon footprint, and the tool used to obtain these numbers.
    - (Optional) if the tool provide it, you can also include: water resources, embodied footprint
- **3) Add the information to the inventory made available to the group members (obligatory)**

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That you have an up-to-date list of the computing hotspots of your group, i.e. the most demanding workflows.
- That you have estimated the energy usage and carbon footprint of each tool in the different use-cases relevant to the team.

**Evidence can include:**
- The up-to-date inventory with the energy and carbon data for each

### Resources
- The [Green Algorithms Initiative](http://www.green-algorithms.org) has tools for carbon estimation of individual workflows. 
- This [publication](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43586-023-00202-5) also summarises the different options
- You can also check out the training resources listed in the “Training” criteria.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that the environmental impacts of each major workflow has been estimated?
- Is the data made available to team members?

## Monitor and report computing usage and environmental impacts

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-compute-silver)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20C4--S-white)

### Impact
Raises awareness, encourages sustainable behaviours, and highlights opportunities for improvement.

### Description
The group reports to its members on computing usage, and associated energy consumption and environmental impacts, either using resources provided by the computing team or using open-source tools.

**How?**

Monitoring of usage and related environmental impacts may already be done by the team operating the computing infrastructure (you can check with them). If not, you can deploy open-source resources yourself, or ask them to do it for you (for example, the [Green Algorithms Initiative](http://www.green-algorithms.org) has some tools for that). If no tool is available for your setup, you can regularly estimate impacts based on overall usage metrics (e.g. manually using online calculators).

This tool should at least track (for each group member): computing usage (e.g. core-hours), estimated energy usage, corresponding carbon footprint. 

Once monitoring is set up in the background, you want to make sure group members know where to find this information and that it gets regularly reported to every group member (at least quarterly).

### Checklist
- **1) Identify if any monitoring tools already exist on the platforms you are using (compulsory)**
- **2) Identify the best monitoring approach to use for your usage, and implement it (compulsory)**
    - Detail how monitoring is done (e.g. which tool is used) and any info you have about the methodology.
- **3) Report these impacts to team members regularly (compulsory)**
    - Share a sample report detailing data on computing usage and environmental impacts
    - Provide evidence this has been shared with group  members, and the frequency of reporting
- **4) Provide evidence showing that your computing pipeline inventory from your Bronze application is maintained, noting the date of last update.**

### Resources and Case Studies
- The [Green Algorithms Initiative](http://www.green-algorithms.org) has open-source resources for computing carbon monitoring.  
- For a quick overview of how to estimate computing carbon footprint, you can check out [this publication](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43586-023-00202-5). 

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That your group monitors computing usage and related environmental impacts
- That these are reported on a regular basis to group members (showing an example)
- That these reports are disseminated to your members.
- That the computing pipeline inventory is maintained, with the date of last update.

**Evidence can include:**
- A PDF file of your report, showing computing usage and environmental impacts within a specific timeframe.
- A screenshot of communications showing this report has been shared with members.
- The updated computing pipeline inventory.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence of a report drafted by the group, detailing computing usage and environmental impacts within a specified timeframe?
- Does the approach used to obtain these numbers look sensible: it will have to be a balance between accuracy and feasibility, but if possible, they should use recognised tools/methodology (you can reach out to the Green DiSC team if you have a doubt on this).
- Is there evidence that this report has been shared with members of the group?

## Include information on resource usage for software released by the group

![Category general](https://img.shields.io/badge/category-compute-silver)
![Criteria ID](https://img.shields.io/badge/Criteria%20ID-(RG)%20C5--S-white)

### Impact
Improves transparency, enables users of these tools to make sustainable choices in how they use them.

### Description
If the group builds software or tools that are distributed for use by others, the documentation should list the expected resource requirements of the tool in some standard use cases. This should at least  include: runtime, memory requirements, data storage, and tradeoffs available to the user and how they impact resource usage (e.g. increasing parallelisation, disabling some flags). If relevant, it should also provide guidelines on how to run it most sustainably (e.g. trading longer runtime for lower energy usage). 

**How?**

You can list the resource usage of standard use cases as well as outline how it scales (e.g. with the dimension of the input). Mention of carbon footprints when appropriate should also be included. This becomes part of the release notes for software/tools built by the group.

### Checklist
- **1) Identify the list of tools that should be included in this (obligatory)**
- **2) Collect the data needed for each (obligatory)**
- **3) Add the information to the public-facing documentation for these tools (obligatory)**
    - Provide evidence that this is available to potential users of your software/tool

### How will this be reviewed?
**Your submission should demonstrate:**
- That you’ve outlined the software/tools developed by your group.
- This documentation outlines supporting details (e.g. runtime, memory requirements, data storage, network usage, etc.).
- This documentation is accessible to potential users of the software/tools.


**Evidence can include:**
- PDF document or spreadsheet listing the software/tools developed by the group, with supporting detail as instructed.
- Screenshot showing that this document is made available to potential users of the software/tools.

### Auditor Guidance
- Is there evidence that the group has listed the software/tools developed?
- Does the public documentation of the tools include information on resource usage and expected carbon footprint?
- Is there evidence showing that the documentation is made available to potential users?
